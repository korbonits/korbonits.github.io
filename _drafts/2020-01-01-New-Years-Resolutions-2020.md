## Closing out a decade; how to start the next

2019 retrospective below my 2020 goals.

Welcome to 2020! I am changing up my new year's resolutions this year. I am doing this because I want to grow and change in some new areas, focus on areas I've let slip in the past, and because I know that I can easily hit my overall reading goals (but can't seem to hit the Great Books goal). 2019 was a wild ride.

For 2020 I want to re-iterate the importance of creating [SMART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMART_criteria) goals. That's:
- Specific
- Measurable
- Achievable
- Relevant
- Time-bound

High-level summary of 2020 resolutions:

- Reading

## Don't read 50 books

Antipattern is too strong of a word, but when I create the goal to read 50 books it weighs on me all year (it doesn't bring me down -- on the contrary -- but it's on my mind). I love to read, and in 2019 I ended up reading more than I ever have. However, reading a certain number of books is too facile, as a goal. By that I mean, I could challenge myself more in my reading goal than the total number of books or number of pages read. In 2020, I plan to read more deliberately.

#### Writing: failure

 - NaNoWriMo: failure
 - Hugo House courses: success/failure
 - Keep writing all year: failure
 - Get published: success/failure

Toward the summer of 2019 I shifted a lot of my time toward studying the fundamentals of computer science, particularly, data structures and algorithms. This is time that was formerly shared between writing and reading in 2018, and in 2019 this time was spent reading and practicing CS fundamentals. Although I'm disappointed that I didn't do NaNoWriMo in 2019, I'd like to return to it at some point. 

In early 2019 I took Fiction II and improved my craft from 2018 further, however, I was not able to participate in Fiction III in the Spring due to family health circumstances, work, sailing, and travel. As I continued to read periodicals, I began to gravitate toward poetry. One goal for 2020 I'd like to persue is to take a course or two in poetry: it seems more natural to me from a compositional perspective than writing fictional prose.

One small win in 2019 was taking flash fiction I wrote in my Fiction III course and expanding/adapting it for the Ploughshares Emerging Writer's Contest. My submission was not selected as a winner, but I enjoyed the process of crafting, responding to peer and instructor feedback, and sumbitting to a very reputable contest. The fiction submissions were overseen by Ottessa Moshfegh, whose fiction I admire greatly.

#### Travel: success

 - Visit one new country: success

 Going to Japan to race in the Takarabune Regatta in Kobe, in Osaka Bay, was one of the highlights of 2019. This was my first visit to Japan and certainly hope it isn't my last. I visited Osaka, Kobe, Himeji, Kyoto, and Tokyo. I spent a week with locals (against whom I was racing in the regatta), raced for a day with an all-Japanese crew, and got to know my fellow yacht club members better. I also spent time with a college friend and time alone exploring Tokyo.

 Top takeaways from Japan: food, trains, castles, and shrines.
